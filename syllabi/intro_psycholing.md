## Course Overview

This course introduces psycholinguistics through direct engagement with the experimental process. Rather than just surveying what psycholinguists have discovered, you'll learn by conducting research yourself: formulating testable hypotheses, designing experiments, collecting data, analyzing results, and communicating findings.

The semester is organized around six stages of experimental research, each focusing on a different linguistic domain. You'll work through the complete pipeline multiple times, building competence with methods, statistical analysis, and scientific communication. **Prerequisites**: Introduction to linguistics or permission. No experimental experience required.

## Learning Objectives

Upon completion, students will be able to:
1. Formulate testable research questions from linguistic theories
2. Design psycholinguistic experiments with appropriate controls
3. Implement experiments using self-paced reading, eye-tracking, acceptability judgments, and priming paradigms
4. Collect experimental data using online platforms (PCIbex, Gorilla)
5. Analyze psycholinguistic data using R and mixed-effects models
6. Interpret experimental results in relation to linguistic theory
7. Write research papers in APA/journal format
8. Critically evaluate published psycholinguistic research

## Course Structure: The Six-Stage Experimental Cycle

### Stage 1: Question Formation (Weeks 1-3)
**Domain**: Speech Perception & Phonological Categories

Students learn to translate theoretical questions into empirically testable hypotheses. We examine categorical perception experiments and design studies testing whether phonological categories are innate or learned.

**Deliverable**: Experimental design proposal (Week 3)

### Stage 2: Literature Review (Weeks 4-6)
**Domain**: Lexical Access & Word Recognition

Students conduct systematic literature reviews using databases and citation tracking. We study lexical competition using the visual world paradigm and priming experiments.

**Deliverable**: Annotated bibliography and synthesis (3-4 pages, Week 6)

### Stage 3: Experimental Design (Weeks 7-9)
**Domain**: Syntactic Parsing & Garden-Path Effects

Students design complete experiments including materials, counterbalancing, fillers, and analysis plans. We examine how working memory constraints affect sentence processing.

**Deliverable**: Full experimental design with materials and predictions (Week 9)

### Stage 4: Data Collection (Weeks 10-11)
**Domain**: Language Production & Agreement Errors

Students implement experiments online and collect pilot data. We investigate agreement attraction in production: why speakers produce errors like "The key to the cabinets are on the table."

**Deliverable**: Pilot data collection report (Week 11)

### Stage 5: Data Analysis (Weeks 12-13)
**Domain**: Statistical Modeling & Theoretical Commitments

Students analyze experimental data, fit mixed-effects models, and interpret results. We examine how statistical modeling choices reflect theoretical assumptions about cognition.

**Deliverable**: Data analysis with visualization (Week 13)

### Stage 6: Scientific Communication (Weeks 14-16)
**Domain**: Writing & Presentation

Students write complete research papers and present findings. We discuss how to communicate experimental results to specialist and general audiences.

**Deliverable**: Final project paper and presentation (Week 16)

## Reading Framework: QALMRI

All paper readings use the QALMRI framework:
- **Question**: What is the research question?
- **Alternatives**: What are competing hypotheses?
- **Logic**: How does the experimental design test between them?
- **Method**: What procedures were used?
- **Results**: What patterns emerged?
- **Inferences**: What do results tell us about theory?

You'll submit 6 QALMRI summaries (1-2 pages each) throughout the semester, replacing traditional exams. This framework helps you read like a researcher, not just a student memorizing facts.

## Required Methods & Tools

Students gain hands-on experience with:
- **Self-paced reading**: Word-by-word reading time measurements
- **Eye-tracking**: Gaze duration and fixation patterns during reading
- **Acceptability judgments**: Gradient grammaticality ratings
- **Priming paradigms**: Lexical and syntactic facilitation effects
- **Online platforms**: PCIbex and Gorilla for web-based experiments
- **Statistical analysis**: R, lme4, and mixed-effects regression models

---

## Final Project

Students design and execute an original psycholinguistic experiment:
- Develop a testable hypothesis grounded in linguistic theory
- Design experimental materials with appropriate controls
- Collect pilot data from participants
- Analyze results using appropriate statistical methods
- Write findings in journal format (12-15 pages, APA style)
- Present study to the class (10-minute presentation)

Projects should demonstrate mastery of the complete experimental workflow.
